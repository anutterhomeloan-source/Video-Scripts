*Built on a real client call (2026-06-27): she deposited $3,000 toward her down payment, then revealed it was a personal loan — which can't be used as funds.*

---

## Hook (Problem)
A client called me this weekend, so excited. She goes, "Jordan, I just put $3,000 toward my down payment!" …And I had to be the one to tell her — we can't use it.

## Branding
I'm Jordan Nutter, and I help make mortgages make sense.

## Empathy
And before you think she did something dumb — she didn't. This trips up so many people. Because nobody explains where your down payment is actually allowed to come from.

## Answer / Story
Here's what happened. That $3,000? She took out a personal loan for it. And I get the logic — money's money, right? But a lender doesn't see it that way. Borrowed money can't be your down payment. Two reasons. One — it's brand new debt. That changes what you qualify for. Two — it has to be *your* money. Saved, or a documented gift. Not a loan.

VISUAL: "Personal loan ❌ can't be your down payment" card (borrow-downpayment-story.png)

## Change
So before you move any money for a down payment — talk to me first. Your own savings? Great. A gift from family? Great, we just document it. A personal loan, a credit card cash advance, money you can't trace? That stops your loan cold.

VISUAL: Checklist — "Counts: ✅ Your savings ✅ Documented gift · Doesn't: ❌ Loans ❌ Cash advances"

## End Result
The good news — she's totally fine. We paused, lined up the real funds, kept moving. But imagine finding that out at the closing table instead.

## CTA
Not sure if your down payment money "counts"? Don't guess — and don't move it yet. Fill out the link in my bio and let's make sure your funds are good to go.
