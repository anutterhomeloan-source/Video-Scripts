## HOOK
If your appraisal comes in lower than your contract price, your deal is not automatically dead. But you need to know your options — fast.

## BRANDING
If you don't know who I am, my name is Jordan Nutter and I help mortgages make sense.

## PROBLEM + EMPATHY
So here's the thing most buyers don't realize. Your lender — aka me — bases the value of the home on the appraisal, not the contract price. So if the appraisal comes in lower, there's a gap. And when buyers see that number, they panic. Which makes sense — nobody tells you this is even a possibility.

We actually had a deal under contract at $535,000 that appraised $60,000 under. That's a big gap. And the seller wouldn't budge, so the deal had to be cancelled. But what I want you to know is — we had options before we got to that point.

**VISUAL:** Story-hook card — "$535,000 contract / $60,000 under appraisal / Here's what happened." (use low-appraisal-card.png)

## ANSWER + CHANGE
When a low appraisal happens, here's what you can actually do:

**VISUAL:** Simple 4-option list overlay — "When the appraisal comes in low…"

- **Option one** — renegotiate the price with the seller. Sometimes they'll come down, especially if they want the deal to close.
- **Option two** — bring extra cash to cover the gap. If you love the home and you have it, this might be worth it.
- **Option three** — request a value reconsideration. If there are better comparable sales your appraiser missed, or information that wasn't included, your lender can push back on that number.
- **Option four** — walk away. And honestly? Sometimes that's the right financial decision.

**VISUAL:** Highlight "value reconsideration" — "Most buyers don't know this exists"
